## 5. Conclusion

We presented MoTA, a model-agnostic test-time adaptation framework that addresses the long-standing domain overfitting problem in image demoiréing. Unlike prior work that either bakes adaptation into a specific architecture or relies on expanding the training data distribution, MoTA decouples adaptation from the backbone network through two lightweight adapter modules — a Fourier Domain Adapter for frequency-domain correction and a Spatial Gating Adapter for spatial modulation. The MASS self-supervision signal exploits moiré's characteristic mid-frequency energy concentration to produce pseudo-clean targets, enabling effective single-image adaptation without paired data. Experiments across four datasets and multiple backbone architectures demonstrate that MoTA delivers consistent cross-domain gains while adding less than 5% to the parameter count and requiring only 15 adaptation steps per image.

Our results point to a broader principle: for degradation types with well-characterized frequency signatures, test-time adaptation informed by domain-specific signal properties can be more effective — and more parameter-efficient — than either architectural innovation or data expansion alone. This principle may extend beyond demoiréing to other structured artifacts such as JPEG blocking, sensor noise patterns, and halftone aliasing.

## Limitations

MoTA inherits two limitations that suggest directions for future work. First, the MASS signal relies on the accuracy of the Moiré Frequency Detector (MFD), which is pre-trained on source-domain data with synthetic moiré annotations. If the target domain's moiré frequency distribution differs substantially from what MFD was trained to recognize, the pseudo-clean target quality degrades, reducing adaptation effectiveness. Online MFD adaptation or unsupervised frequency anomaly detection could mitigate this sensitivity. Second, the current framework operates on single images independently, without leveraging the temporal consistency available in video capture. Adapting MoTA to exploit inter-frame constraints — for instance, sharing adapter states across adjacent frames or using motion-compensated MASS signals — would be a natural extension for video demoiréing applications. Finally, while our evaluation covers four diverse datasets, real-world deployment would benefit from broader testing across an even wider range of device types, screen technologies (OLED, microLED), and capture conditions (varying lighting, multi-scale capture).
